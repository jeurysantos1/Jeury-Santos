In one line

In 2026, Meta's Ads Manager App set out to make its advertisers reachable — to reliably deliver the billing, policy, and performance alerts they can't afford to miss. Millions had no verified email or phone on file. As lead product designer — partnering with PM, engineering, content, and fellow designers — I shaped how we'd close that gap on mobile. This is my part in that.

The problem

Notifications that never land

When I picked up this problem, the symptom looked simple: critical notifications weren't reaching advertisers. Underneath, the cause was that their contact info was missing, stale, or unverified — so the alerts quietly failed.

The hard part wasn't designing a form. The consent and verification experience — opt-ins, one-time codes, legal copy — is owned by a web surface, and rebuilding it natively would have been costly and slow to keep compliant. But advertiser intent lives in the native app. So I framed the real question I had to answer: how do I surface the entry point natively while reusing the web verification stack — without the seam between them feeling broken?

Hypothesis

If we add a native entry point on AMA settings…

…and route users to the existing mobile-site contact flow via the in-app browser, we can increase verified contact coverage without building a full native flow.

✓

Increase verified coverage

Advertisers in Settings see contact info as a first-class item — not buried in notifications.

✓

Reuse the mobile-site flow

The web contact collection already handles email, phone, and WhatsApp verification — no rebuild.

✓

In-app browser feels native

Confirmed with the platform team: the Facebook in-app browser stays in-app — no Safari redirect.

The approach

"Native add, holding" + in-app browser handoff

I weighed three architectures — pure native, pure mobile-site, and a hybrid — and chose the hybrid. The entry point lives natively, but add + verify happen on the existing mobile site inside a Facebook in-app browser that slides up. The trick I kept coming back to: the native screen never navigates away — it holds behind a dimmed backdrop, then returns with its state updated. I treated the native app as the durable host and the web view as a transient task surface, because that let me reuse the compliant stack while keeping it feeling native.

Strategy — when & where to prompt

Not just a screen — a prompt strategy

Once the surface worked, the tension I had to resolve was adoption vs. disruption. My answer was progressive disclosure — I never fire every entry point at once, and I match each one to the user's intent and lifecycle.

Where — tiered urgency by surface

Tier 1 · Always on
Account Settings

Why — zero disruption. Users here are already in "setup mode," so the prompt belongs. It's the passive baseline that catches anyone looking to configure their account.

Frequency · always visible

Tier 2 · Contextual
Notification tray

Why — best adoption surface. The user is already there, checking notifications about their ads. Only fires if there's no WhatsApp 7 days after sign-up — never to brand-new users still onboarding.

Frequency cap · 5 days

Tier 3 · Last resort
Home banner

Why — most disruptive. They came to work, not configure — so it's a last resort, used only after Tier 1–2 are ignored, and always one-tap dismissible with "Don't show again."

Frequency cap · 10 days

→One entry point at a time — if the banner shows, the tray card is suppressed.
→7-day cooldown after any dismiss before re-showing on any surface.
→"Don't show again" is permanent — kills the banner + tray; Settings always stays.

When — by user lifecycle

Frequent in setup, patient when busy, conservative when disengaged. Tier 1 always fires on Day 0.

Process & key decisions

The calls I made — and why

I worked in fast loops — explore, prototype, align, decide — and brought each contested call to the team with evidence instead of opinion. A few that mattered:

1

Empty-state badge

I explored five row treatments and cut the redundant "Not yet added" badge — "None" plus the add button already say empty. I kept a single badge, Pending confirmation, because it's the one that pulls users back to finish.

2

Tap-target color

Research pointed to gray — FB/IG/iOS reserve blue for links, not tap surfaces. The team chose blue for AMA consistency, so I documented the trade-off we were accepting rather than treat it as settled. Influence ≠ authority.

3

Pattern research

I wrote an FB/IG/iOS pattern analysis so we'd settle debates by precedent, not opinion — covering tap targets, add affordances, badging, edit patterns, and empty states.

The flow

One verification engine, many channels

I built email, phone, and WhatsApp on one verification engine — the same form → code → verify pipeline, parameterized per channel — with loading, success, and error states, and chaining to the next empty channel. End to end, it's 21 screens across 3 channels; here's the path an advertiser actually walks:

1

Discovery

From Settings, they open Contact information and see email, phone, and WhatsApp status at a glance.

2

Collection

Tapping a channel opens the mobile site in the in-app browser to enter the contact and opt in — then a code is sent.

3

Verification

They enter the code, it auto-validates, and the flow auto-advances to the next unverified channel.

4

Completion

Back on the native widget, channels read "verified" — and notifications finally reach them.

Drag to pan · pinch or ⌃-scroll to zoom · double-click to reset

Final design — approved

Geo 2.0 rows, truthful state, effortless add

I designed the contact rows to lead with the channel name and show the value beneath it, with a state-specific action: a blue "+" to add, "⋮" to manage a pending channel, "✎" to edit a verified one. I kept it consistent across AMA and the Mobile Site so the pattern travels.

Outcome

✓ Final design approved to move forward

I made each call deliberately and documented the trade-offs — and the end-to-end prototype I built de-risked the flow, aligning engineering and content on real behavior instead of static comps. The design was approved to build.

How success is measured

↑ Contact add-rate ↑ Verification completion ↑ Notification deliverability

Guardrails: "Don't show again" / opt-out rate and CSAT must stay flat — adoption can't come at the cost of annoyance. The tier cadence is a hypothesis I'd tune with a holdout and per-segment dismissal data post-launch.

Reflections

What I'd carry forward

Decide the variables separately

Splitting "tap-target color" from "badge treatment" into two clean comparisons made alignment fast — agree on one without relitigating the other.

Convention informs, the team decides

Precedent didn't dictate the answer (we kept blue) — but it made the call deliberate and defensible.

Prototype the seams

The hardest part of a hybrid native↔web flow is the handoff. Building it surfaced — and let me fix — issues a static mock never would.

Less, but meaningful, signal

Removing the redundant empty badge let the one status that matters — "Pending confirmation" — actually pull attention.

My one takeaway The leverage wasn't a better screen — it was choosing where the work should live. Reusing the web stack and letting the native app simply host it is what made this shippable, compliant, and fast. I'll reach for "borrow, don't rebuild" far earlier next time.
What's next Ship Tier 1 (Settings) first, instrument reach + opt-out, then tune the tiered cadence with a holdout. I'd also generalize the verification-handoff pattern so other Meta surfaces can reuse it — the real payoff is a reusable pattern, not one feature.

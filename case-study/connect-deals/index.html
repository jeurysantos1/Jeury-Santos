The problem

The black box of deal reporting

Reporting answered "what happened" but never "what now." Three pain points kept surfacing.

01

Efficiency drain

Sellers spent an estimated 12,000 hours a year on manual workarounds — downloading raw data into spreadsheets just to understand deal health.

02

Lack of actionability

Reporting was too high-level to answer basic client questions like "How close are we to the next tier?" — a true black box.

03

Alarm fatigue

Overuse of red alert colors for non-critical metrics caused anxiety and desensitization — eroding trust in the reporting system.

Research

Uncovering the seller's reality

Discovery interviews with 17 participants across 4 global regions (AMER, EMEA, APAC, LATAM), plus concept-validation sessions with 8 users. I audited the current system for cognitive load across metric hierarchy, navigation, insight visibility, and external shareability.

01

The "now" bias

Sellers focused on current milestones — they cared most about the action they needed to take right now.

02

Manual calculations

Many were manually calculating gap-to-goal metrics in Excel because the tool wouldn't.

03

Fear of sharing

Sellers hesitated to share their screen with clients — nothing separated internal metrics from client-safe ones.

The insight

The problem wasn't the data — it was interpretation time. Sellers aren't analysts; the dashboard has to synthesize "spend X to get Y," not just display numbers.

"I need to know if my client is leaving money on the table so I can call them today."

Strategy

Design principles for a wall of data

  • Focus on the gap. Emphasize gap-to-next-tier in the current milestone, not just total spend.
  • Visuals over tables. Replace text-heavy tables with interactive donut charts for spend segmentation.
  • Dual-access experience. A simple view for quick checks; a detailed view for deep troubleshooting.

Three requirements were non-negotiable:

  • Metric visibility — draw the eye to the metrics that matter most.
  • External safety — a toggle that hides internal data from the client-facing view.
  • Dynamic filtering — slice spend by buying platform (DV360, Google Ads, YouTube) in one click.

How we got here

Three directions, one decision

Before committing, I tested how much the dashboard should carry — would a minimalist view drive faster action, or do sellers need the detail to trust the data?

Option A

Detailed

Every metric on screen — Forecast Tier, Forecast YTC, Forecast DIF.

Trade-off: complete, but cognitively heavy — the signal gets lost in the noise.

Option B

Minimalist

Only what's urgent — current pacing and gap-to-next-tier.

Trade-off: calm and fast, but too thin for sellers to fully trust the numbers.

Option C · Selected

Hybrid

Defaults to All Metrics, but visual hierarchy surfaces pacing and milestone progress while de-emphasizing the rest.

Why it won: detail on demand, clarity at a glance — sellers trust it and still act fast.

One more test. Reframing "Credits at Risk" from a red alert into a neutral metric measurably reduced anxiety — confirming the second hypothesis.

Final design

One screen that answers "what now?"

A milestone summary card tracks current-quarter performance; a visual spend deep-dive replaces static tables; and an external-share toggle makes the whole view client-safe.

Usability testing & iterations

What we tested — and fixed

  • Grouping. Users preferred the detailed view but wanted related metrics grouped to cut clutter.
  • Color logic. Standardized pacing colors — green (>100%), yellow (90–100%), red (<90%).
  • The red problem. Changed "Credit at Risk" to neutral gray unless risk was critical, reducing alarm fatigue.
  • Chart confusion. Switched vertical bars to horizontal timelines to match the seller's mental model of progress over time.
  • Toggle placement. Moved "External Share" from settings to the top-level view, next to the deal title.

Reflection & next

Data density isn't the enemy — poor hierarchy is

  • Sellers wanted more data, not less — but organized by urgency so it became actionable.
  • Sellers aren't analysts. Data must be synthesized ("spend X to get Y"), not just displayed — and trust is visual: sellers judge accuracy by how professional the report looks.
  • Next: generative AI for performance summaries — auto-writing the "performance summary" sentence so sellers focus on selling, not interpreting.
